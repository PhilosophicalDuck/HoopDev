## 1. Ball Handling Drills

Ball handling is the foundation of every position. The goal of solo dribbling work is to make the ball feel like an extension of your hand so that in game situations your brain can focus entirely on reading the defense.

### Stationary Drills

**Spider Dribble**
- Stand with feet slightly wider than shoulder width, knees bent
- Alternate dribbles in front of each leg: right front, left front, right back, left back — cycling continuously
- Do not look at the ball
- Sets/reps: 3 x 30 seconds
- Builds: finger strength, ambidexterity, hand-eye dissociation

**Figure 8 (Single Ball)**
- Weave the ball in a figure-8 pattern through and around both legs
- Start slow to establish the path, then accelerate
- Variation: no-look figure 8, one dribble between each pass-through
- Sets/reps: 3 x 30 seconds each direction
- Builds: coordination, weak-hand comfort, change-of-direction feel

**Low/High Dribble Switches**
- Dribble as low as possible (6 inches off the ground) for 10 reps, then as high as waist level for 10 reps, alternating
- One hand at a time, then alternate
- Sets/reps: 4 x 20 switches per hand
- Builds: ball control at varying heights, protective dribble foundation

**Two-Ball Simultaneous**
- Dribble both balls at the same time at the same height
- Progression: alternating (one up, one down), then crossovers with both balls, then one high/one low
- Sets/reps: 3 x 45 seconds each variation
- Builds: independent hand coordination, forces brain to handle split attention

**Tennis Ball Dribbling**
- Dribble a basketball with your weak hand while tossing and catching a tennis ball with your strong hand
- Eyes stay up tracking the tennis ball
- Sets/reps: 3 x 60 seconds
- Builds: eyes-up dribbling habit, peripheral awareness, ambidexterity under cognitive load

### On-the-Move Drills

**Cone Weave (Full Court)**
- Set 6-8 cones in a straight line approximately 5 feet apart
- Attack each cone with an inside-out dribble or crossover before the next cone
- Variations: between-the-legs at every other cone, behind-the-back, spin move
- Sets/reps: 5 full-court trips
- Builds: change-of-direction speed, dribble moves at game pace

**Two-Ball Full Court**
- Dribble two balls simultaneously from baseline to baseline
- Alternating dribbles only; must stay under control
- Sets/reps: 5 full-court trips
- Builds: coordination, conditioning combined with handles

### Progression Framework

| Level | Focus | Drills |
|-------|-------|--------|
| Beginner | Stationary control, one hand | Figure 8, low/high switches, Spider |
| Intermediate | On-the-move, eyes up | Cone weave, tennis ball, two-ball stationary |
| Advanced | Game speed, combo moves | Two-ball full court, chair work, resistance band |
| Elite | Cognitive load + handles | Tennis ball + defender reads, music tempo changes |

## 2. Shooting Drills

Shooting is the most practiced skill in solo basketball work because it is the most measurable and the most impactful. The key principle across all levels: quality of repetition matters more than quantity. Every shot should have a defined target, consistent footwork, and a full follow-through.

### Form Shooting

**One-Hand Form Shot (The Foundation)**
- Stand 3-5 feet directly in front of the basket
- Use only your shooting hand — guide hand rests at your side
- Focus on: consistent shot pocket (ball at forehead height before release), elbow directly under the ball, full wrist snap, goose-neck follow-through held for 2 seconds
- Zero miss policy before stepping back: 10 makes in a row, then move back 2 feet
- Sets/reps: 50 makes total, starting at 3 feet
- Builds: the mechanical foundation that every other shooting drill rests on

### The Steph Curry Pre-Game Routine (182 Shots)

Documented by journalist Tim Kawakami (Mercury News, 2015) and widely observed by NBA reporters, Curry's pre-game routine targets 120 makes out of approximately 182 attempts. The structure:

1. Layups (both hands, game-speed cuts) — 2 minutes
2. Form shots at 5-8 feet — 15 makes
3. Mid-range pull-ups from 3 spots (elbow left, top of key, elbow right) — 15 makes each spot
4. Catch-and-shoot from 5 spots on the three-point line — 10 makes per spot
5. Off-the-dribble threes from each wing — 10 makes per side
6. Deep threes (27-30 feet) — 10 makes
7. Full-speed transition threes — 10 makes
8. Half-court free throws to close

The core principle Curry emphasizes is identical footwork on every single catch — the feet must be set before the ball arrives in the shot pocket.

### The Kobe Bryant "Mamba" Shooting Method

Documented by trainer Tim Grover and teammates:
- 4 AM workouts with 800 makes as a session goal on certain days
- 15 specific spots on the floor, rotated systematically
- One-hand form shooting at 6 inches from the basket before every session — zero exceptions
- Pull-up mid-range from both elbows: the "Kobe Spot" (mid-range from the elbow) was the highest percentage shot in his arsenal

### Spot Shooting

**5-Spot Circuit**
- Choose 5 spots: left corner, left wing, top of key, right wing, right corner
- Take 10 catch-and-shoot attempts from each spot
- Track makes. Record your total (out of 50) each session
- Progression: add off-the-dribble at each spot once you hit 70% catch-and-shoot
- Sets/reps: 2-3 full circuits per session
- Builds: shooting consistency across all zones, shot-zone tracking data

**Ray Allen Baseline-to-Baseline Spot System**
- Start at the left block, work counterclockwise around the three-point line to the right block
- Each spot: 5 catch-and-shoot makes before moving to the next
- Simulate a self-pass by tossing the ball out to yourself and catching into your shooting motion
- Builds: complete floor coverage, footwork consistency on every catch

### Pull-Up and Off-the-Dribble Work

**Two-Dribble Pull-Up**
- Start at the three-point line, attack toward the elbow with 2 hard dribbles, pull up
- Alternate sides each rep
- Sets/reps: 4 x 10 per side
- Builds: pull-up shot off live dribble, stops, balance at the point of release

**One-Dribble Side-Step**
- Catch at the wing, take one hard dribble laterally, step into the shot
- Used heavily by KD and Kawhi Leonard
- Sets/reps: 4 x 10 per side
- Builds: space creation off one dribble, lateral footwork

### Shot Clock Simulation

Set your phone timer to 5-second countdowns. Catch the ball (toss to yourself), you must release before the alarm. Forces game-speed decisions and eliminates the habit of over-settling.

## 3. Footwork & Athleticism

Great footwork separates good shooters from great ones. Solo footwork work requires no ball and can be done anywhere with space.

### Pivot Work

**Front Pivot Series**
- Catch or imagine catching a pass at the wing
- Practice front pivot to face the basket from both left and right foot as pivot
- 10 reps each foot x 3 sets
- Builds: legal pivot habits, creating angles to drive or shoot

**Reverse Pivot Series**
- Same setup, but spin away from the basket into a face-up position
- Common in post-entry situations and mid-range pull-ups
- 10 reps each foot x 3 sets

### Jab Step Series

- Triple threat position (low, ball in shot pocket)
- Jab right → hesitate → drive left (5 reps)
- Jab right → hesitate → shoot (5 reps)
- Jab right → hesitate → jab again → crossover drive (5 reps)
- Mirror everything on the left side
- Builds: triple threat reads, creating separation before first dribble

### Defensive Slide Drills

**Full-Court Zigzag Slides**
- Start at corner, defensive slide to half court in a zigzag pattern (slide, drop-step, slide the other direction)
- Stay low, do not cross feet, stay on balls of feet
- 5 full-court trips
- Builds: lateral quickness, defensive hip positioning

**Mirror Drill (Without Partner)**
- Set two cones 10 feet apart
- Slide from cone to cone, touching each cone with outside hand, never crossing feet
- 3 x 30 seconds as fast as possible
- Builds: lateral explosion, defensive conditioning

### Agility Ladder

**Icky Shuffle** — in, in, out pattern down the ladder
**Lateral In-Out** — both feet in each box, lateral movement direction
**Ali Shuffle** — feet straddle outside the ladder, hop feet inside each box alternating

Sets: 3 passes through each pattern
Builds: foot speed, coordination, ankle stability

### Plyometrics for Basketball

| Exercise | Sets x Reps | Basketball Benefit |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Box Jumps (18-24 inch box) | 3 x 8 | Vertical explosion, landing mechanics |
| Lateral Bounds | 3 x 10 each side | Single-leg lateral power for drives and cuts |
| Depth Jumps | 3 x 6 | Reactive strength (plyometric landing) |
| Broad Jump | 3 x 6 | First-step explosion |
| Ankle Hops (both feet) | 3 x 20 | Calf conditioning, bounce |

Rest 90 seconds between plyometric sets. Never do plyometrics while fatigued — quality of ground contact degrades and injury risk rises.

## 4. Finishing at the Rim

### Basic Layup Package

Practice these in order until each is automatic:

1. Right-hand layup (straight in)
2. Left-hand layup (straight in)
3. Right-hand layup from left angle
4. Left-hand layup from right angle
5. Reverse layup (right side, lay to the left side of the backboard)
6. Reverse layup (left side, lay to the right side)

Sets/reps: 5 makes each variation before moving to the next

### The Mikan Drill (Foundation of Finishing)

Named after George Mikan. Considered mandatory for any serious big or guard developing a finishing package.

- Stand under the basket
- Shoot a right-hand hook shot off the right side of the backboard
- Without letting the ball hit the ground, catch it and immediately shoot a left-hand hook off the left side
- Continue alternating without stopping
- Sets/reps: 3 x 20 makes (10 each side)
- Progression: Reverse Mikan (same drill but using the back of the backboard on reverse layups)

Kareem Abdul-Jabbar confirmed in interviews that the Mikan Drill was his foundation before extending to the jump hook and eventually the sky hook.

### Euro Step

- Attack the basket at full speed
- Plant right foot, step left (or plant left, step right) to clear an imaginary defender
- Lay the ball up on the opposite side from your last step
- Key: the gather step before the two-step sequence is legal under NBA/NCAA rules
- Sets/reps: 4 x 10 each direction
- Builds: finishing through traffic, contact avoidance, body control

### Floater

- Attack the lane at a 45-degree angle
- At the free-throw line or just inside, release the ball with a soft arc above the projected block point
- One-hand release (teardrop) or two-handed push (runner)
- The ideal release point puts the ball above where a shot-blocker's hand can reach (8-10 feet high)
- Sets/reps: 4 x 10 each side
- Builds: finishing over length in the paint

### Power Layup

- Gather at the charge circle with two feet
- Absorb contact, do not go around it
- Finish through with strength, not finesse
- Sets/reps: 5 x 10 reps each side
- Builds: contact finishing, drawing fouls

## 5. Post Game Drills

### Drop Step

- Catch (imagined) at the low block with back to basket
- Read which side the defender is on
- Drop the foot on the defender's side, pivot with the other foot, explode to the rim
- Left block drop step: drop left foot baseline, spin, right-hand layup
- Right block drop step: drop right foot baseline, spin, left-hand layup
- Sets/reps: 5 x 10 each side
- Builds: the most fundamental post finish in basketball

### Up-and-Under

- Begin with the drop-step footwork
- Fake the shot (sell it: ball goes up, head goes up, body rises slightly)
- When defender rises for the block, duck under and finish on the other side
- Sets/reps: 5 x 10 each side
- Builds: reading shot-blockers, counter move off the drop step

### The Dream Shake (Hakeem Olajuwon System)

Hakeem's post game originated from his soccer background in Lagos, Nigeria — specifically the footwork patterns he used as a goalkeeper. He later taught this system to Shaquille O'Neal, Dwight Howard, and dozens of other big men.

**Core principle:** Sell the fake completely. The defender's weight shift is your trigger — you go the opposite direction of their lean.

Solo practice method:
1. Catch at the block, back to basket
2. Fake baseline (sell completely: ball, eyes, shoulder all go baseline)
3. Counter into the lane with a spin move or jump hook
4. Alternate which side you receive on each rep

Sets/reps: 3 x 15 each side
Builds: post counters, reading defensive weight shifts, patience in the post

### Face-Up Game from the Post

- Catch at the elbow or mid-post with back to basket
- Front pivot to face the defense
- From face-up: jab-step series (drive, shoot, pass read)
- Solo: practice the jab → pull-up or jab → drive finishing off either foot
- Sets/reps: 4 x 10 from each elbow

### Jump Hook to Sky Hook Progression

1. **Jump hook**: Two-step gather, one-foot takeoff, release at the top of the jump arc — same-side hand as shooting foot
2. **Extended jump hook**: Same but extend the release arm to full reach before releasing
3. **Sky hook**: Running one-step gather from the mid-post, full arm extension at the apex, ball released at maximum height

Start with the Mikan Drill for the hook motion before attempting game-speed sky hooks.

## 6. Solo Passing Drills

Passing improves even without a partner through wall work. The goal is to develop quickness of release, accuracy, and feel for every pass type.

### Wall Passing Drills

**Chest Pass**
- Stand 8-10 feet from a wall, step into each pass, catch the return
- Focus: two hands on release, push through the ball, fingertip control on the snap
- Sets/reps: 3 x 30 passes

**Bounce Pass**
- Aim the pass to bounce approximately two-thirds of the distance to the wall
- Useful pass in traffic; develops feel for pass angles
- Sets/reps: 3 x 30 passes

**Overhead Pass (Outlet)**
- Start overhead, release with snap, catch the return
- Simulates outlet passes and skip passes over pressure
- Sets/reps: 3 x 20 passes

**One-Hand Push (Skip Pass Simulation)**
- One-handed release, pushed from the hip or shoulder level
- Simulates skip passes across the floor
- Sets/reps: 3 x 20 per hand

**Reaction Timing Drill**
- Stand 6 feet from the wall, release the ball, react to the return as fast as possible
- No pause between catch and release — continuous
- Sets/reps: 3 x 45 seconds at maximum pace

### Why Solo Passing Matters

Passing under pressure is a trainable skill. The wall return forces you to handle the ball at angles you did not set up — simulating unpredictable passes in games. The quicker your hands and the softer your catch, the more composed your game decisions become.

## 7. Conditioning Drills

### 17s

**Format:** Run sideline-to-sideline 17 times (touching each line) in under 60 seconds.

Used as a fitness benchmark by NCAA programs and some NBA teams. 17 sideline-to-sideline touches = approximately 510 feet of lateral running in one set.

- Sets: 3-5 with 90-second rest between
- Builds: anaerobic capacity, lateral conditioning, mental toughness under fatigue

### Suicides (Full Court)

**Format:** Baseline → free-throw line back → half court back → far free-throw line back → far baseline back. That is one suicide.

- Sets: 5-10 with 60-second rest
- Target time: under 30 seconds per suicide for competitive players
- Builds: sprint conditioning, acceleration/deceleration mechanics

### Full-Court Defensive Slide Circuit

- Baseline to half court: zigzag defensive slides
- Half court to far baseline: sprint (simulating a fast break)
- Far baseline back: back-pedal to half court, then sprint to origin
- Sets: 5 full trips
- Builds: defensive conditioning that doubles as footwork skill work

### Jump Rope Protocol

3 rounds x 2 minutes:
- Round 1: standard two-foot alternating (warm-up pace)
- Round 2: single-leg alternating (30 seconds left, 30 seconds right x2)
- Round 3: double-unders (maximum pace)

Rest 60 seconds between rounds.
Builds: calf explosiveness, ankle stability, rhythm — all directly transferable to basketball footwork.

### Combining Conditioning with Skill

The most efficient conditioning for basketball happens when skills are embedded:

| Drill | Conditioning Effect | Skill Effect |
|-------|--------------------|----|
| Full-court two-ball dribble | Aerobic + anaerobic | Ball handling |
| Mikan Drill (continuous, 60 seconds) | Anaerobic burst | Finishing |
| Spot shooting (game-speed, no rest) | Aerobic + footwork | Shooting |
| Zigzag slides + sprint | Lateral conditioning | Defensive footwork |
| Suicide + layup finish | Full-sprint conditioning | Finishing under fatigue |

## 8. Mental Reps & Visualization

### How Elite Players Use Visualization

**LeBron James**
Documented in multiple interviews to spend the morning of game days visualizing specific game scenarios: defending a particular player, receiving a specific play call, and finishing in transition. He describes the process as "scripting" — running the game before it starts so nothing surprises him.

**Kobe Bryant**
The night before games, Kobe would mentally script entire defensive schemes: which move he would use against a specific defender in the second quarter, how he would counter a trap at the elbow. He described visualization as the way he "solved" games before they happened. He also used it to reinforce shooting mechanics — mentally rehearsing his release point before going to sleep.

**Steph Curry**
Curry has a pre-shot breath-and-reset routine in warm-ups and games. Before each shooting sequence, he takes a short breath, visualizes the ball going through the net, and then shoots. He consistently describes confidence as "trusting the work" — the mental connection between practice repetitions and in-game belief.

### How to Practice Visualization (Solo)

**Pre-session mental warm-up (3-5 minutes)**
- Sit or lie quietly, eyes closed
- See yourself performing the specific drills you are about to do — perfect form, make after make
- Feel the ball in your hands, the sound of the net, your feet set correctly
- This primes the motor patterns you are about to activate

**Shot routine anchor**
- Choose a consistent pre-shot routine: one bounce, look at the rim, breath, shoot
- Every single shot in practice — form shots, game-speed, free throws — must go through the exact same routine
- The routine becomes the mental trigger for focused execution

**Post-session review (2-3 minutes)**
- After practice, mentally replay the best 3 plays or makes from the session
- Reinforce what went well, not what went wrong
- Going to sleep with positive replay consolidates the motor memory overnight

### Deliberate Practice (Anders Ericsson Framework)

Anders Ericsson's research on expert performance identified four elements of deliberate practice, all of which apply directly to solo basketball work:

1. **Defined goal** — not "shoot around" but "make 50 shots from the left elbow, tracking makes/attempts"
2. **Expert feedback** — use video self-review, coaching apps (AnalyzeMyShot), or a coach to identify form breakdowns
3. **Full concentration** — no music at first, no distraction; every rep with complete focus
4. **Working outside the comfort zone** — drill the weak hand, the weak shot zone, the move you avoid in games

The key insight: 10,000 hours of unfocused repetition does not create expertise. 1,000 hours of deliberately structured, feedback-rich practice does.

## 9. Daily Solo Workout Structure

A complete solo session should be 75-90 minutes. Below is a time-blocked template.

| Phase | Duration | Content |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Warm-up | 10 min | Light jog, dynamic stretches, ankle hops, arm circles, 3 minutes form shooting at 3 ft |
| Ball Handling | 15 min | Spider (2 min), Figure 8 (2 min), Two-ball stationary (3 min), Cone weave full court (5 min), Tennis ball (3 min) |
| Shooting | 25 min | Form shots 5-10 ft (5 min), Spot shooting 5-spot circuit (10 min), Pull-ups from both elbows (5 min), Off-dribble threes (5 min) |
| Footwork & Finishing | 15 min | Mikan Drill (3 min), Euro step / Floater (5 min), Jab-step series (4 min), Pivot work (3 min) |
| Conditioning | 10 min | 5 suicides OR 3 x 17s OR full-court slide circuit |
| Mental Reps / Cool-down | 5 min | Stretch, visualize best plays from session, set goals for next session |

**Total: 80 minutes**

### Adjustments by Time Available

| Time Available | Remove | Keep |
|---------------|--------|------|
| 45 minutes | Conditioning, half of ball handling | Shooting (15 min), Finishing (15 min), Form shots (10 min), Brief warm-up (5 min) |
| 60 minutes | Reduce conditioning to 5 min, reduce ball handling | All other phases |
| 90+ minutes | Nothing | Add post game work or second shooting circuit |

## 10. Weekly Periodization Plan

### Off-Season Template (Maximum Development)

| Day | Focus | Intensity |
|-----|-------|-----------|
| Monday | Full session: Ball handling + Shooting heavy | High |
| Tuesday | Footwork + Post game + Conditioning heavy | High |
| Wednesday | Active recovery: light shooting, mental reps, stretching | Low |
| Thursday | Full session: Finishing package + Shooting | High |
| Friday | Conditioning + Ball handling + Plyometrics | High |
| Saturday | Long shooting session (game speed, shot clock simulation, spot work) | Medium-High |
| Sunday | Full rest | — |

### In-Season Template (Maintenance)

During a season, solo work must not create fatigue that degrades performance. Volume drops, intensity stays high.

| Day | Focus | Duration |
|-----|-------|----------|
| Day before game | Light form shooting, mental visualization only | 20 min |
| Game day | Pre-game shooting routine (Ray Allen style spot work) | 15-20 min |
| Day after game | Active recovery only — light ball handling, stretching | 20 min |
| Off day (mid-week) | One full solo session targeting a specific weakness | 60 min |
| Off day (late week) | Shooting-only session | 45 min |

### Progression Principles

- Every 4 weeks, add one new move or drill variation to avoid adaptation plateaus
- Track one benchmark metric per week (e.g., spot shooting percentage, 17s time, Mikan Drill consecutive count)
- Every 8 weeks, do a deload week at 50% volume — this is when the body consolidates gains

## 11. Progress Tracking & Measurement

### Core Benchmark Sheet

Track these weekly. Record date, number, and conditions (court surface, fatigue level).

| Metric | How to Measure | Baseline Goal | Elite Goal |
|--------|---------------|---------------|------------|
| Spot shooting % (5 spots x 10) | Makes / 50 attempts | 30/50 (60%) | 40/50 (80%) |
| Free throws consecutive | Max makes in a row | 10 | 25+ |
| Mikan Drill (60 seconds) | Alternating makes in 60s | 20 | 35+ |
| 17s time | Time for 17 sideline touches | Under 70s | Under 58s |
| Two-ball full court | Trips without losing a ball | 3 | 8+ |
| Pull-up makes (both elbows, 20 shots) | Makes / 20 | 10/20 | 16/20 |

### Video Review Protocol

Filming your own solo sessions is the single most effective form of self-coaching without a coach present.

**Setup:** Phone on a tripod at mid-court level, angled to capture your full body and the basket.

**Review checklist:**
- Shooting: Is the elbow under the ball? Is the wrist snapping fully? Is the follow-through held?
- Ball handling: Are your eyes down? (Bad.) Are they up? (Good.)
- Footwork: Are feet set before the ball arrives on catch-and-shoot?
- Finishing: Is the ball above the rim at the release point?

**Tools:**
- AnalyzeMyShot.com — submit video clips for coach analysis
- Hudl Technique (free app) — slow-motion frame-by-frame review on your phone
- Coach Dave Love's ShotMechanics YouTube channel — use as comparison reference

### Setting Benchmarks

Every 4 weeks, take 10 minutes at the end of a session to run through the benchmark sheet with fresh data. Plot the numbers on a simple spreadsheet. Looking at 3-month trends reveals what is improving and what has stalled — and what to prioritize next.
